The Path to Greatness is Paved in Blood

03 Apr 2014

introduction

I am not the best fighting game player. But I'm also not the average player. I have not won any majors. I've won a couple of local tournaments here and there but nothing else that would distinguish me from any other tournament player. But even so, becoming an above-average skill player was not by luck.

Through my experiences I have traveled to many tournaments and gathered enough knowledge to know what it takes to be more than just another player.

In this article I will explain what it takes to rise above the average skill level in your area and why certain players in your community always seem to dominate the scene despite having played X game less than you or make it look like they are naturally that good.

Becoming great at anything requires equally great effort. Unless you are immensely talented this will mean hours of deliberate practice and deep focus. No matter what it is you want to improve at just remember that in the grand scheme of things, hard work and effort trumps talent. Only in the highest level of competition does talent even become a factor worth mentioning. To put into numbers we can say that being great requires 90% hard work, 5% talent, and 5% luck.

In other words, your path to greatness is paved in blood.

The Correct Mindset

The mindset of a great player exudes confidence. Without confidence you cannot follow through with your actions in good faith.

The mindset of a great player is infused with positive thinking. Every positive thought put into action is a step closer towards greatness. This includes setting goals and taking positive steps towards achieving them. Without this a player will let their frustrations get the best of them hindering their path to greatness.

The mindset of a great player is objective. These players are critical of their own play and make adjustments according to impartial and unbiased evaluation. Feedback, whether it is positive or negative, is important in facilitating this effort.

These are the core components that I believe make up great players.

Learning to Lose

Sometimes players will lose. The difference is how the player reacts to their losses. A great player cannot be passive about their losses whereas an average player is more likely to be. A great player sees losses as small stories hidden with lessons yet to be learned. They take their losses in full, seldom blaming the result on factors other than things they can control.

"When you lose, don't lose the lesson."

Preparing to Win

Great players take effort in their preparations towards winning. To win you must prepare to do so. This where a huge amount of your hard work will be put towards in terms of improving at fighting games. The preparation towards becoming great at X fighting game can be broken into a few steps:

Learn the game's system and mechanics. Pick and learn a character. Learn how this character works under the game's mechanics. Acquire knowledge to test out (training mode, reading, watching match-videos or replays). Apply knowledge by actually playing "real matches". Discard what didn't work and keep what did. Repeat steps 4-5 as needed.

Step 1 through 3 should be fairly straightforward. Intimate knowledge of how the game actually works goes hand-in-hand with how well a character can play. Learning a character includes learning spacing with their normals, learning how good their core mechanics are (reversals, rolls, backdash, short-hop, etc.), bread and butter combos from different hit confirms, and so forth. Steps 4 and 5 hold the biggest weight. Acquiring knowledge and knowing how to apply it properly is key. If you stick with just playing real matches and don't explore much of your character in training mode then your potential for improvement is substantially smaller compared to someone who does.

Knowledge fuels growth. A balance between training mode and matches facilitates this growth.

Deliberate Practice

Training mode allows you to find answers to any question you may have about your character. Don't know any safe block strings? Test it in training mode and apply it the next time you have a real match. Experiment. Never stop learning things about your character.

Those players in your area that make it seem effortless and don't seem to play as much is simply because they know how to invest their time towards improvement. The number of hours you practice does not amount to anything if it is not towards the right kind of practice. Training mode isn't just for learning combos and repeating them ad infinitum. Combos can lead to wins, but just as important is creating those opportunities which allow you to perform them.

Deep Focus

What you are aiming for through the acquisition of knowledge is to minimize any unfamiliarity during any match. You should always be in control when you play your matches. What this means is constant awareness of your options at any moment in any match. Training mode helps confirm what these options might be. If you encounter any unfamiliar situations in a match you should recreate this situation later and explore your options to fully understand what is going on.

In Summary

Improving at fighting games takes the right kind of effort. While I have not won a major (yet!) I think what I have written here is the foundation for a player that might one day win one.
